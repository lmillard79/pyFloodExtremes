In flood frequency analysis (FFA) and extreme value modeling, statistical frameworks rely on fitting a probability distribution to a dataset using a parameter estimation (or inference) method 1-3. The sources detail four primary inference methods within these frameworks: the Method of Moments (including EMA), Maximum Likelihood Estimation, L-moments, and Bayesian Inference.  
**1\. Method of Moments and the Expected Moments Algorithm (EMA)**The traditional Method of Moments (MOM) estimates parameters based on the statistical moments (mean, variance, and skew) of a sample 2\. While simple, MOM is highly sensitive to outliers and has largely been superseded by more robust methods 4\. To overcome its limitations with complex data, the **Expected Moments Algorithm (EMA)** was developed as a generalized method of moments 5, 6\. Adopted in US federal guidelines (Bulletin 17C), the EMA provides a uniform statistical framework to directly fit distributions (such as the Log-Pearson Type 3\) using the entire dataset 6, 7\. It allows the simultaneous integration of systematic records, historical and paleoflood information, regional skew, and censored or interval data, while systematically adjusting for potentially influential low floods (PILFs) 6, 8\.  
**2\. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)**MLE is a robust classical standard in statistical inference due to its theoretical motivation and asymptotic efficiency 2, 9, 10\. It determines the parameter values that maximize the likelihood of observing the given data 11\. However, MLE has notable drawbacks in extreme value analysis:

* It typically provides only a point estimate (the mode of the likelihood function) rather than a full distribution of parameter uncertainty 12\.  
* To describe uncertainty, MLE often relies on asymptotic normality assumptions (such as the Fisher Information Matrix and quadratic approximations of the log-likelihood) 12, 13\. The sources demonstrate that these first-order asymptotic approximations can severely underestimate the true uncertainty of flood quantiles—especially for skewed distributions like LP3 14-16.  
* When complex measurement errors are introduced, the likelihood function can exhibit multiple optima, making single-mode MLE estimates meaningless for probabilistic risk assessment 17\.

**3\. L-Moments and LH-Moments**As discussed previously, L-moments use linear combinations of order statistics to provide unbiased estimators that are far less sensitive to outliers than traditional product-moments 18, 19\. While L-moments (and the LH-moments generalization) offer a simple and highly effective framework for fitting models to standard gauged data 20, 21, they are limited in complex scenarios. They are restricted primarily to gauged discharge data and cannot easily incorporate regional prior information, historical data, or systematic rating curve errors without requiring special adjustments 20\.  
**4\. Bayesian Inference**Bayesian inference is presented as the most flexible and comprehensive statistical framework for FFA 22-25.

* **The Framework:** Instead of producing a single point estimate, the Bayesian approach treats the parameters themselves as random variables 26\. It combines a *prior distribution* (which can incorporate regional information or expert knowledge) with the *likelihood function* of the observed data to compute a *posterior distribution* using Bayes' Theorem 22, 23, 25, 26\.  
* **Implementation:** Because evaluating the posterior distribution analytically is often impossible, Bayesian frameworks rely on intensive Monte Carlo techniques. For example, the software FLIKE uses *Importance Sampling*, while RMC-BestFit utilizes *Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)* methods to sample from the posterior distribution 27-29.  
* **Advantages:** By generating a full posterior distribution, Bayesian methods overcome the limitations of MLE's asymptotic assumptions, producing accurate, often asymmetrical credible intervals for flood quantiles and expected damages 12, 16, 30\. It seamlessly integrates complex uncertainties—such as interval data, perception thresholds, paleoflood records, and measurement/rating curve errors—directly into the likelihood function 25, 31-33.  
* **Regional Frameworks:** The Bayesian framework is also highly effective for Regional Flood Frequency Estimation (RFFE). Using **Bayesian Generalised Least Squares (BGLS)** regression, regional models can accurately separate time-sampling errors from true underlying model errors. This avoids the unrealistic negative model error variances that can occur with classical estimators 34-36.
